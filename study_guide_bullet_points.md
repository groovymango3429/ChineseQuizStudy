# Modern Chinese Philosophy - Study Guide Bullet Points

## 1. Zhang Zhidong: The Three Necessities for China's Survival

- **Three Core Necessities**: Zhang Zhidong identified maintaining the state, preserving Confucian doctrine, and protecting the Chinese race as essential for China's survival
- **Hierarchical Relationship**: The three elements are interconnected - to protect the race, one must preserve the doctrine; to preserve the doctrine, one must maintain the state
- **Knowledge as Foundation**: Zhang believed that preserving the race requires knowledge and education as fundamental tools
- **Military Strength**: He emphasized that Confucian doctrine is maintained through strong armies and military power
- **Power and Doctrine Link**: Without imperial power, the doctrine cannot be properly followed or protected
- **Ti-Yong Framework**: Zhang promoted "Chinese learning for essence (ti), Western learning for utility (yong)"
- **Reform Philosophy**: He advocated selective modernization while maintaining traditional Chinese values
- **Educational Reform**: Supported establishing modern schools that combined Western science with Confucian ethics
- **Self-Strengthening Movement**: Was a key figure in late Qing reform efforts during the 1860s-1890s
- **Conservative Modernizer**: Sought to preserve traditional Chinese culture while adopting Western technology and methods
- **Practical Approach**: Emphasized practical learning over pure theory to strengthen China
- **Cultural Defense**: Viewed Western learning as tools to defend Chinese civilization, not replace it

## 2. Kang Youwei: His Understanding of Confucius, Interpretation of Confucianism, and Stance Toward Family

- **Confucius as Reformer**: Kang portrayed Confucius as a progressive reformer rather than a conservative traditionalist
- **Reinterpretation of Classics**: He challenged orthodox interpretations of Confucian texts, arguing many were forgeries
- **Three Ages Theory**: Adapted Confucian concepts into stages of historical progress - Age of Disorder, Age of Small Peace, and Age of Great Unity
- **Datong (Great Unity) Vision**: Envisioned a utopian future of universal harmony and equality based on Confucian principles
- **Institutional Reform**: Advocated for constitutional monarchy and radical institutional changes in China
- **Family Structure Critique**: Proposed revolutionary changes to traditional family structures in his utopian vision
- **Abolition of Family**: In his work "Datong Shu," he suggested eventual abolition of the traditional family system
- **Gender Equality**: Advocated for women's rights and gender equality, radical for his time
- **Universal Education**: Proposed universal public education to raise children collectively
- **Marriage Reform**: Suggested temporary marriage contracts and freedom in relationships
- **Evolutionary Confucianism**: Combined Confucian thought with Western ideas of social evolution
- **Hundred Days Reform**: Led the failed 1898 reform movement attempting to modernize Qing China

## 3. Liang Qichao: Renewing People, Public Morality, Nationalism vs. Culturalism/Universalism

- **New Citizen Concept**: Advocated for creating "new citizens" with modern civic consciousness and public spirit
- **Private vs. Public Morality**: Distinguished between traditional Chinese private morality and the need for public morality
- **Public Morality Deficit**: Argued that China excelled in private morality but lacked public morality necessary for modern nation-states
- **Civic Virtue**: Emphasized civic duties, public responsibility, and participation in political life
- **Nationalism Development**: Evolved from culturalism to embrace nationalism as necessary for China's survival
- **Cultural Universalism**: Initially promoted Chinese culturalism emphasizing civilizational superiority over racial or national identity
- **Shift to Nationalism**: Gradually shifted toward modern nationalism in response to foreign imperialism and internal crises
- **Nation-State Building**: Recognized the need for a strong nation-state identity in the modern world
- **Education for Citizenship**: Believed education should cultivate citizens rather than just moral individuals
- **Political Participation**: Advocated for constitutional government and citizen participation in politics
- **Social Darwinism Influence**: Incorporated ideas of national competition and survival of the fittest
- **Media and Reform**: Used journalism extensively to spread ideas about political and social reform

## 4. Yan (Yen) Fu: Faith/Value vs. State/Country, Sage in the West and in China, His Understanding of Freedom

- **Translation Work**: Famous for translating Western works including Adam Smith, John Stuart Mill, and Herbert Spencer into Chinese
- **Faith and State Distinction**: Explored the relationship between personal values/beliefs and loyalty to the state
- **Western Sage Concept**: Analyzed how Western thinkers served as "sages" through scientific and philosophical inquiry
- **Chinese Sage Tradition**: Contrasted Western emphasis on knowledge with Chinese sage focus on moral cultivation
- **Freedom as Self-Discipline**: Interpreted Western liberty not as license but as self-discipline and following natural laws
- **ç¾¤ (Qun) Concept**: Emphasized group solidarity and collective strength while balancing individual freedom
- **Social Evolution**: Introduced Spencer's evolutionary theories to China, applying them to social development
- **Wealth and Power**: Argued that Western nations succeeded through cultivating individual energy within collective frameworks
- **Scientific Method**: Advocated for adopting Western scientific approaches to problem-solving
- **Three Fundamental Bonds**: Discussed differences in Western emphasis on liberty, equality, and democracy versus Chinese values
- **Gradual Reform**: Unlike radical reformers, favored gradual change based on education and cultural transformation
- **Practical Liberty**: Viewed freedom in practical terms as the ability to develop one's potential within social constraints

## 5. The Relationship Between Substance (Body/Ti) and Function (Use/Yong)

### Ancient Meaning
- **Classical Ti-Yong**: Originally a metaphysical concept distinguishing essence (ti) from its manifestation (yong)
- **Buddhist Influence**: Concept developed through Buddhist philosophy distinguishing absolute truth from phenomenal reality
- **Organic Unity**: Traditionally understood as inseparable aspects of a single reality
- **Metaphysical Foundation**: Ti represents the underlying principle or essence; yong represents practical application

### Zhang Zhidong's Interpretation
- **Chinese Learning as Ti**: "Chinese learning for essence, Western learning for utility"
- **Cultural Preservation**: Used ti-yong to justify adopting Western technology while preserving Chinese values
- **Conservative Framework**: Maintained Confucian ethics as unchangeable essence (ti)
- **Selective Modernization**: Western science and technology as tools (yong) to strengthen Chinese civilization

### Kang Youwei's Interpretation
- **Reinterpreted Framework**: Challenged the rigid separation between Chinese essence and Western utility
- **Dynamic Confucianism**: Argued that Confucianism itself could be the ti for both Chinese and universal values
- **Progressive Application**: Saw Confucian principles as compatible with Western institutions and reforms
- **Unified Vision**: Sought to transcend the ti-yong dichotomy by showing Confucianism's universal applicability

### Liang Qichao's Interpretation
- **Integrated Approach**: Initially accepted ti-yong framework but gradually questioned its limitations
- **Beyond Dichotomy**: Recognized that Western learning included both substance and function
- **Holistic Reform**: Argued that China needed to adopt Western political and social institutions, not just technology
- **Synthetic Vision**: Sought to synthesize Chinese and Western thought rather than maintain rigid boundaries

### Yan Fu's Interpretation
- **Critical Analysis**: Questioned whether substance and function could truly be separated
- **Systemic Understanding**: Argued Western strength came from integrated systems of thought, not just techniques
- **Liberty as Essence**: Suggested that Western liberty and individualism were the true ti behind Western power
- **Coherent Systems**: Emphasized that Western success came from coherent philosophical systems, not isolated tools
